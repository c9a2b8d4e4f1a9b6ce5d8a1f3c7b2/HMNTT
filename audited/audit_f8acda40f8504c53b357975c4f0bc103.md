### Title
Unbounded Retry-Backoff Amplification via BGP Black-Hole in `buildNetworkFromMirrorNodes`

### Summary
`buildNetworkFromMirrorNodes` is invoked with `context.Background()` (no deadline) and retries `httpClient.Do()` up to 11 times with uncapped exponential backoff. An attacker controlling a BGP route to the mirror REST endpoint can silently drop all packets, causing each attempt to hang for the full `mirrorNodeClientTimeout` (10 s default), then wait through exponentially growing backoff periods, producing a worst-case startup stall of ≈621 s — far exceeding any reasonable block-time threshold.

When packets are black-holed, the TCP SYN receives no reply and no RST/ICMP, so Go's HTTP stack waits silently until the client-level timeout fires.

**Worst-case total stall (default config):**

| Component | Value |
|---|---|
| 11 attempts × 10 s timeout | 110 s |
| 10 backoffs (500 ms, 1 s, 2 s … 256 s) | ≈511.5 s |
| **Total** | **≈621.5 s** |

**Why existing checks fail:**
- `http.Client.Timeout` bounds each individual request but not the aggregate.
- The `ctx.Done()` select in the backoff only responds to OS signals (SIGINT/SIGTERM) — not to any wall-clock deadline.
- There is no `context.WithTimeout` wrapping the entire `buildNetworkFromMirrorNodes` call.

### Impact Explanation
The pinger cannot complete startup for ≈621 s, blocking all monitoring transaction submissions. No `/tmp/ready` file is written until `newClient` returns, so liveness/readiness probes fail. The delay (621 s) is orders of magnitude beyond 500% of Hedera's ~3–5 s consensus round time (~25 s threshold). A sustained BGP black-hole keeps the pinger permanently non-functional by restarting the stall on every pod restart.

### Likelihood Explanation
BGP prefix hijacking is a documented, repeatable real-world attack (e.g., the 2018 Amazon Route 53 hijack, the 2010 China Telecom incident). An attacker with access to a transit or peer AS that carries routes to the mirror REST host's IP prefix can inject a more-specific route. This does not require any credentials on the target system. The attack is repeatable: every pod restart re-triggers the full stall.

### Recommendation
1. Wrap the entire `buildNetworkFromMirrorNodes` call with a bounded context:
   ```go
   totalCtx, cancel := context.WithTimeout(ctx, cfg.mirrorNodeClientTimeout * time.Duration(cfg.mirrorNodeClientMaxRetries+1) * 2)
   defer cancel()
   netmap, err := buildNetworkFromMirrorNodes(totalCtx, cfg)
   ```
2. Cap the exponential backoff (e.g., `min(backoff, 30*time.Second)`).
3. Pass the signal-aware `ctx` from `main` rather than `context.Background()`.

### Proof of Concept
1. Deploy the pinger with `HIERO_MIRROR_PINGER_NETWORK=other` and `HIERO_MIRROR_PINGER_REST=http://<target-ip>:5551`.
2. On a BGP router upstream of `<target-ip>`, advertise a more-specific prefix for that IP with a null-route next-hop (black-hole community `65535:666`).
3. Observe: each `httpClient.Do()` in `fetchMirrorNodeNetwork` hangs for 10 s, then the loop sleeps 500 ms, 1 s, 2 s … 256 s between retries.
4. Total observed startup delay: ≈621 s. The pinger never writes `/tmp/ready` during this window, and no monitoring transactions are submitted.
5. Sustain the BGP advertisement across pod restarts to keep the pinger permanently non-functional.
