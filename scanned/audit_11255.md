# Privileged Roles and Ownership

## Summary
This bug report is about the `BountyV1.sol` file in a protocol. The client has acknowledged the issue and explained that the contract owner will be a multi-sig, meaning that users will have to trust them. The problem is that the privileged roles in the contract, such as the owner and approvers, have the power to control the contract and its functions. This puts users at risk of being attacked if these roles are compromised or malicious. The report outlines different scenarios where a malicious or compromised owner or approver could exploit the system for their own gain. The recommendation is to document these risks and consider using a multi-sig or timelock feature to prevent exploitation.

## Details
**Update**
Marked as "Acknowledged" by the client. The client provided the following explanation:

> The owner of the contract will be a multi-sig and it will be clear via off-chain documentation that the user must place some trust in Arkham.

**File(s) affected:**`BountyV1.sol`

**Description:** The protocol owner and approvers serve privileged roles and can control the configurations of the contract or execute specific functions. Therefore, users are exposed to the risk of being attacked if the privileged roles are malicious or compromised.

**Exploit Scenario:**

**A malicious or compromised owner of `BountyV1` can:**

1.   Grant or revoke the approver role for a specific address, including themselves.
2.   Set the maker or taker fee to any value, including 100%. In the worst case, if the maker fee is 100%, the user completing the bounty will not receive any reward. If the taker fee is 100%, the user will not receive their reward or stake.
3.   Set the submission stake to an arbitrary value. If the stake is set to 0, the spamming protection of the protocol is effectively disabled. If the stake is set to an extremely high value, it may cause a DoS since no one will have such a high stake to make a bounty submission.
4.   Set the fee receiver address to any address, including themselves.
5.   Sunset the contract anytime to make ongoing bounties unable to receive submissions anymore.

**A malicious or compromised approver of `BountyV1` can:**

1.   Approve arbitrary or incorrect bounty submissions, including those submitted by themselves using a different address, and potentially gain the bounty for profit.
2.   After a user reveals their bounty submission off-chain, reject the user's correct submission and re-submit the solution off-chain and on-chain to gain the bounty for profit.
3.   Accept a user's submission even if it is incorrect, causing the bounty poster to payout the bounty for free.
4.   Reject a user's submission even if it is correct, causing the user completing the bounty to give out their solution and stake for free.
5.   Not accepting or rejecting a bounty submission and wait the bounty to expire to make the poster unable to receive a correct submission before the deadline. This is a DoS attack that the approver can do to cause harm to the bounty poster.

**Recommendation:** Consider documenting the risk and impact a compromised privileged role can cause on the protocol and inform the users in detail. As the privileged roles can be the single point of failure of the protocol, consider using a multi-sig or a contract with a timelock feature to mitigate the risk of being compromised or exploited.
