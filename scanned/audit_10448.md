# Sandwich Attacks and Price Manipulation

## Summary
The report discusses a bug in the `swapTokensForETH()` function in the `Reach.sol` file. This function currently allows for any amount of ETH to be returned, making it vulnerable to sandwich attacks. These attacks involve an attacker front-running a victim's trade by buying the same asset and then selling it back at a higher price. The team recommends updating the `swapTokensAtAmount` function to make these attacks less economically attractive.

**File(s) affected:**`Reach.sol`

**Description:** A common attack in DeFi is the sandwich attack. Upon observing a trade of asset X for asset Y, an attacker front-runs the victim trade by also buying asset Y, lets the victim execute the trade, and then executes another trade after the victim by trading back the amount gained in the first trade. Intuitively, one uses the knowledge that someone’s going to buy an asset, and that this trade will increase its price, to make a profit. The attacker’s plan is to buy this asset cheaply, let the victim buy at an increased price, and then sell the received amount again at a higher price afterward.

The swap performed in `swapTokensForETH()` currently accepts any amount of ETH in return, making it vulnerable to sandwich attacks.

Additionally, there have been cases with similar token contracts. If the token is listed in DEXs, an attacker could force the token contract to swap its balance in one pool which would lower its price, buy the token low in that pool, and sell it high in the second pool.

**Recommendation:** Sandwich attacks are hard to prevent reliably without a user-defined minimum output amount or an Oracle-computed price. A possible mitigation would be to update `swapTokensAtAmount` to keep the trade order size low relative to the pool's liquidity to make such attacks economically less attractive.
